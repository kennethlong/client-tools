Presets are grouped properties that can be applied to sounds. Each property has a check box signifying this preset affects that property, and a value to set the property to. Some properties are enabled together, e.g. the volume properties.

Properties can be set to either a numeric value, or a variable name.

Filters

Presets also are responsible for applying filters to sounds. There are 8 filter stages that can be set. By default a preset does not affect the filter. To remove an existing filter, select "Remove Existing" for the relevant filter stage.

When you select the desired filter, the properties for the filter are displayed. These can be set to variables as well.

Properties

  • Volume, Pan, Center, LFE - these are decibel and left/right front/back scalars for the sound.

  • Playback rate scale - this lets you speed up or slow down playback. In semitones.

  • Reverb wet level - this lets you control how much reverb is applied during playback. Decibels. To hear environmental reverb, you'll have to move this slider beyond its default setting of -96! This is basically a volume control for the sound's reverb path.

  • Reverb dry level - this lets you control how much of the original sound is left during playback. Decibels.

  • Built-in LPF cutoff - this lets you turn on the super-fast low-pass-filter that's available on every sound. In Hertz.

  • Occlusion - this controls how `occluded` the audio will sound. Occlusion means the extent to which the sound source is completely blocked - a sound playing in another room separated by a wall, for example. This setting is usually not saved in a preset but rather controlled directly by the game engine (although you can turn it on to audition and test the effect).

  • Obstruction - this controls how `obstructed` the audio will sound. Obstructions imply that the direct path of the sound is blocked, but an indirect path is still available - a sound playing from behind a crate, for example. This setting is usually not saved in a preset but rather controlled directly by the game engine (although you can turn it on to audition and test the effect).

  • Exclusion - this controls how `excluded` the audio will sound. Exclusion means the extent to which the sound's direct path is clear but its reverberations are blocked - a sound playing outside a room and heard through an open window, for example. This setting is usually not saved in a preset but rather controlled directly by the game engine (although you can turn it on to audition and test the effect).

  • Spatialization - this controls whether the sound will use its 3d position to sound like its coming from that direction, relative to the listener.

  • 3D minimum distance - since sound effects should always be normalized, you'll need to use the minimum distance to make each sound `feel correct` as it approaches and moves away from the listener. For example, a bumblebee could have a minimum-distance of 0.25 while a jet plane might have a minimum distance of a hundred meters. By default, each time the distance between the listener and the sound is halved, the volume of the sound is doubled. This means that any sound within the minimum distance plays at the maximum volume, while beyond the minimum distance, the volume becomes one-half as loud each time the distance from the listener to the minimum-distance boundary is doubled. Only the minimum distance controls the falloff curve -- when the maximum distance is reached, the sound will continue playing, but its volume will be forced to zero.

  • 3D maximum distance - this establishes a hard clip point where the volume is forced to zero. This setting doesn't affect the volume falloff - it just hard clips to zero when reached.

  • 3D wet reverb attenuation - specifies whether the reverb on this sound (if any) should be scaled by distance to the listener as well.

  • 3D cone inner angle - specifies the interior angle of the audible sound cone. If the listener is within this angle, the volume will be normal.

  • 3D cone outer angle - specifies the outer angle of the audible sound code. If the listener is outside this angle, then the sound volume will be scaled by the cone's outer volume setting. As the source-listener angle moves from the inner angle value to the outer angle value, the volume is gradually scaled to approach the outer volume.

  • 3D cone outer volume - sets the volume attenuation outside the 3D outer angle. Decibels.
